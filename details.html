Research Overview

Foundation models are rapidly moving from controlled research into critical real-world infrastructure in robotics, clinical decision support, industrial inspection, and edge devices, where environments shift, failures carry consequences, and compute is constrained. Yet a deep gap remains between impressive AI demonstrations and safe, robust deployment. Closing this gap is not merely a technical challenge; it is essential for AI to realise its transformative potential.

Real-world deployment exposes three fundamental limitations in current AI systems: limited adaptiveness (models remain static and require costly retraining), insufficient trustworthiness (poor calibration and overconfident predictions in high-stakes settings), and resource inefficiency (reliance on monolithic architectures).

My research addresses these challenges through three directions: Adaptive AI, Trustworthy AI, and Resource-Efficient AI, unified through the concept of a Modular AI System (MAS). Rather than treating AI as monolithic, MAS reconceptualises it as interacting, independently configurable components built around a shared core. This framework enables fine-grained adaptation, well-calibrated behaviour, and efficient deployment within a unified architecture.

Research Directions
Unified by Modular AI System (MAS)
Direction 1
Adaptive AI

Deployment environments are inherently dynamic. A diagnostic AI may encounter rare disease presentations never seen in training; a robot may navigate an unseen environment; an industrial monitor may face entirely new fault signatures. Most foundation models remain static after training — lacking mechanisms to update, self-correct, or generalise without expensive retraining or fine-tuning.

Within the MAS framework, I design components that can be independently updated or replaced without disturbing the rest of the system. This enables fine-grained, continual adaptation: updating task-specific reasoning without retraining perception, and seamlessly delegating sub-tasks to specialised expert components.

Direction 2
Trustworthy AI

High-stakes applications — clinical decision support, autonomous systems, infrastructure monitoring — demand more than accurate predictions. They require reliability, calibrated uncertainty, and predictable behavior. Overconfident failures in these settings can have severe, irreversible consequences.

My work develops principled calibration and uncertainty quantification methods for modular systems, with guarantees that hold across operating conditions. By isolating uncertainty within targeted components of MAS, the system can selectively activate high-capacity modules only when uncertainty demands it — achieving reliability without sacrificing efficiency.

Uncertainty Quantification Calibration Safety Decision-Making Under Uncertainty
Direction 3
Resource-Efficient AI

A standard large language model demands orders of magnitude more compute than available on edge hardware. Deploying AI under real-world constraints on compute, memory, latency, and cloud connectivity requires principled compression and specialisation — not just post-hoc pruning.

The MAS architecture naturally enables resource efficiency: components can be compressed and specialised independently, and the system can dynamically adjust its compute footprint based on task complexity and available resources. This achieves the flexibility of large-scale models within the footprint of resource-constrained hardware.
